Beyond the sheltered valley of delight,<br />
Our pilgrim ventured into lands unknown.<br />
Soon verdant fields gave way to blighted sight:<br />
Charred remnants of forests blackened and prone,<br />
Villages in ruin, roofs caved and charred,<br />
And silence where once life’s vibrance was sown.<br />
This was a land where hope had been marred,<br />
Where war or plague left marks of misery;<br />
Stench of despair in air heavy and tarred.<br />
He wrapped the plain brown shawl about gently,<br />
Hiding his glowing robe of inner peace,<br />
To walk among the lost as no one but he.

In a hamlet scorched, his steps did decrease;<br />
He saw gaunt faces peering from huts torn,<br />
Eyes hollow, suspicious, begging surcease.<br />
From house to house, burnt shafts of wheat forlorn<br />
Stood charcoaled in fields where famine held reign.<br />
Children cried feebly, weak from empty morn.<br />
The pilgrim’s heart clenched seeing so much pain:<br />
Here wisdom’s lofty light seemed far away,<br />
Hunger of flesh overcame hunger of brain.<br />
He approached a rubble well where they say<br />
A few villagers gathered to draw<br />
Muddy water for their thirst day by day.

As he neared, their voices hushed into awe,<br />
Not reverent but wary of the strange,<br />
For travelers were rare since ruin did gnaw.<br />
He offered greeting, careful not to change<br />
Their comfort by any holy pretense.<br />
He asked what happened, what made fortune estrange.<br />
An elder with face of grief deep and dense<br />
Answered slow, voice raspy from smoke and fear:<br />
“Plague came first, then soldiers crossing from hence<br />
To elsewhere; they burned all and left us mere<br />
To fend among ashes of kith and kin.<br />
We survive barely; death ever is near.”

One woman with babe too weak to grin<br />
Looked up with desperate hope and hollow eyes:<br />
“Do you bring aid or care? Our lives grow thin.”<br />
The pilgrim nodded, sorrowed by their cries.<br />
Not a doctor nor wealthy lord was he,<br />
Yet something from his journey might suffice.<br />
“I carry little, yet what I have, see—”<br />
He reached into satchel and produced bread,<br />
Unbroken loaves three from Beloved City.<br />
He gave them out to hands trembling in dread,<br />
And to the well, went to draw water fresh;<br />
He had seen near valley a stream’s riverbed.

Boiling the water to cleanse death’s thresh,<br />
He helped them drink, and those too weak he fed.<br />
From house to house he moved, driven enmesh<br />
By compassion, caring not how he bled<br />
From cuts or exhaustion or hunger pangs.<br />
Day turned to dusk as he ministered ahead.<br />
Though no miracle, except comfort and simple charity,<br />
A spark lit in eyes where hopelessness hangs.<br />
At night they offered him sanctuary<br />
In a hut half-standing, to share their rest.<br />
He sat by a small fire; they huddled free.

Under broken roof they felt strangely blessed;<br />
The pilgrim told them of no city grand<br />
But offered prayers and songs at their behest.<br />
A lullaby from his childhood land<br />
He sang soft, as mothers nod in relief,<br />
And children for a moment dreams command<br />
Chasing butterflies instead of grief.<br />
In that gentle timbre tears quietly fell<br />
From war-scarred souls, cathartic healing brief.<br />
They asked who he was, why he chose to dwell<br />
With destitute strangers in a wasteland.<br />
He answered humbly, truth in what he’d tell:

“I was once lost in darkness, ‘til a hand<br />
Led me through forests and deserts of doubt.<br />
Now I follow an inner light’s command<br />
To go wherever despair lies about<br />
And simply share what gifts I’ve received:<br />
Kindness, knowledge, hope’s seed to sprout.<br />
My name matters not, nor could be believed,<br />
I’m but a seeker learning to give back<br />
For all the mercy that my soul relieved.”<br />
They marveled, yet further questions did stack,<br />
So he spent the evening recounting mild<br />
Tales of wisdom gleaned on his mystic track.

He spoke of hunger that can’t be exiled<br />
By food or drink, a thirst of soul and mind;<br />
How emptiness and form in union smiled.<br />
Though much was abstruse, they found him kind<br />
And slept more peaceful with him in their midst,<br />
As if his presence left balm left behind.<br />
At dawn he rose to leave, yet they insist<br />
He stays longer, fearing what lies beyond.<br />
But he, on path, by duty’s call was kissed.<br />
He promised to send help from where is fond—<br />
Maybe the City of the Beloved’s aid,<br />
Though distant, their goodwill might respond.

He left amidst thanks and blessings prayed,<br />
Continuing north where mountains did loom.<br />
Each step a question: Could all this be stayed—<br />
These cycles of suffering, violence, doom?<br />
He had seen the heights of human spirit,<br />
Now the depths of our woe in contrast fume.<br />
How to bridge the gulf? Could love clear it?<br />
He remembered the Sufi’s admonition:<br />
Not for self alone is the holy writ.<br />
Trial and sorrow refine our mission,<br />
As fire to gold or chisel to stone,<br />
So compassion must grow from vision.

While crossing rocky passes ragged grown,<br />
He encountered bandits famished and cruel—<br />
Fleeing those wars but predating alone.<br />
They beset him, five men armed as a rule,<br />
Demanding coin or bread or life thereby.<br />
The pilgrim had naught but his inner jewel.<br />
He calmly met their gaze and did not lie:<br />
“I have no gold, only a loaf to eat,<br />
Which I will gladly share though small supply.”<br />
The ruffians, perplexed by this man they meet<br />
Who showed no fear and offered freely bread,<br />
Lowered weapons though desperate minds compete.

One snatched the loaf and split it to shred,<br />
Devouring half, giving half to others.<br />
They then argued if to strike him dead—<br />
For what use a man with empty druthers?<br />
One with scarred face spat, “He’ll slow us, no gain.”<br />
Another, younger, “He could guide like brothers.<br />
He seems a holy beggar, maybe slain<br />
If left alone in these mountains by foes.”<br />
The leader sneered, “Peace, boys, keep your brain.<br />
We took his food; leave him to vultures and crows.”<br />
They turned to go; the pilgrim raised his voice,<br />
Something compelled him, compassion that grows:

“My friends,” he called, making an earnest choice,<br />
“You have my bread, but I see deeper need.<br />
Your eyes speak troubles—can we not rejoice<br />
In finding common solace though we bleed?<br />
I too have walked through sorrows and through strife;<br />
If you will listen, I offer a creed.”<br />
They paused, amused perhaps by captive life,<br />
To humor him or bored upon the road.<br />
He seized the moment to defuse their knife.<br />
He spoke of a city where love bestowed<br />
And exiles and strangers were welcome all,<br />
Where one’s past misdeeds no longer corrode.

He saw in their stances some anger stall,<br />
The younger looked down shame-faced at his blade.<br />
“Many a man,” said pilgrim, “has heard the call<br />
To start again despite mistakes he’s made.<br />
If hunger drives you, there are kinder ways<br />
Than taking from others in fear’s charade.<br />
You have strength and wit—perhaps this displays<br />
You could protect rather than prey upon.<br />
Come with me forward; see if hope conveys.”<br />
They muttered among them: trust not yet drawn,<br />
But seeds of doubt in their path had been sown.<br />
Finally, scar-face grunts, “our time is gone.”

They left him alive, perplexed and windblown,<br />
Heading elsewhere as pilgrim watched them pass.<br />
He sighed, wishing grace had been more clearly shown.<br />
Not every soul is ready to amass<br />
The insight to turn from violence to grace.<br />
Some require time or further horrors amass.<br />
He prayed softly for each bandit’s soul space,<br />
That one day their hunger too find the light,<br />
Then resumed journey at an even pace.<br />
Over crags and high valleys tinted slight<br />
With autumn’s palette he wandered long,<br />
Helping as he could each suffering wight.

He tended the lost, guided with a song<br />
A crazed soldier away from a cliff’s edge,<br />
Shared his cloak with a widow night wind strong,<br />
Spoke calming word to a youth with raw dredge<br />
Of nightmares from battles he’d fled afar.<br />
To each person he gave from wisdom’s hedge<br />
One leaf of truth to heal their inner scar.<br />
Some scoffed or ignored, but many a face<br />
Lit with faint hope as though seeing a star.<br />
And even when progress seemed slow in pace,<br />
Each act of kindness lit two lives at least—<br />
The giver and receiver share one grace.

Yet the valley’s trials had not yet ceased.<br />
As he entered a gorge of red-stone walls,<br />
A swirl of dust forewarned nature not pleased.<br />
The earth groaned low and sudden darkness falls—<br />
A rockslide! Boulders from on high cascade.<br />
The pilgrim barely jumps as death befalls.<br />
Stones crash behind him in a roaring raid,<br />
Trapping him in the gorge with no retreat.<br />
Forward the only path now he must wade.<br />
With staff in hand, he moved wounded feet,<br />
Checking for fractures, but seemed all intact,<br />
Saved for bruises and cuts minor concrete.

He thanked providence for life’s continued tact<br />
And pressed on through narrow canyon’s heart,<br />
Cliffs looming close as if heavens they propped.<br />
The path grew dim; high walls a roof impart<br />
That only noon’s sun could briefly pervade.<br />
In gloom he walked like wandering in art<br />
Of Caravaggio—darkness with light bade<br />
Only at edges where a hope glimmers.<br />
He thought of Plato’s cave and those who stayed,<br />
Chained in shadow, mistaking flicker for whispers.<br />
How different this canyon where he chose to roam<br />
Armed with inner light, though night shivers.

At a bend, he found an old man alone,<br />
Collapsed by the wall, breathing faint and hard.<br />
Rushing forth, the pilgrim in gentle tone<br />
Checked for wounds and gave water from his gourd.<br />
The old man’s eyes opened milky and wide,<br />
His garb tattered, he clearly traveled far.<br />
“Water…” he croaked; the pilgrim did provide,<br />
Lifting the flask to cracked lips of white.<br />
After slow sips, he regained some pride.<br />
“Thank you,” he said, voice raspy as midnight.<br />
He tried to stand, faltered—our friend held fast.<br />
“Rest, father,” said the pilgrim, “no need for might.”

Tears from the elder’s eyes fell thick and fast.<br />
“Not father… just a fool chasing folly.<br />
I left my home for treasure unsurpassed.<br />
I have nothing—ambition ate me wholly.<br />
If not for you, I’d perish in this hell.”<br />
The pilgrim saw guilt o’er his face like ivy.<br />
“All paths teach,” he said, “and mistakes do tell<br />
What truly matters to heart and spirit.<br />
Your life yet is spared; thus use it well.”<br />
The old man wept, “I chased a myth, a writ<br />
In ancient text of a relic divine—<br />
The Sword of Solomon, if one could find it,

Would grant dominion o’er life’s design,<br />
Wisdom and power beyond mortal ken.<br />
I lost companions, spent fortunes supine,<br />
Seeking that blade through mountain and glen.<br />
And now, empty-handed, near death I lie,<br />
My quest in ruin, memory a fen.”<br />
He coughed, and the pilgrim wiped tears dry.<br />
“Gently, friend,” he soothed, “your value’s not lost<br />
Though false treasure lured your years to die.<br />
The wisdom you sought cannot be exhaust<br />
From an object or single secret lore;<br />
It lives in each trial and tempest crossed.”

The man sighed, “My life wasted and deplore;<br />
I left my family, my children grown old<br />
Without their father—what was it all for?”<br />
“Perhaps,” said pilgrim, “the story untold<br />
Is not over yet. You breathe, thus you live.<br />
Return home, mend bonds, let love you enfold.”<br />
The old man nodded weakly, hope a sieve<br />
But slowly refilling drop by drop.<br />
“I shall try if strength and fortune give.”<br />
The pilgrim gave bread and stayed until stop<br />
Of day, when old man felt enough renewed.<br />
Together they left canyon at sun’s drop.

Parting at crossroads, the elder subdued<br />
Thanked the pilgrim as if an angel came.<br />
Each resumed journey by destiny’s feud.<br />
Our pilgrim onward, the valley to tame,<br />
Climbed out of gorge to plateaus ahead,<br />
Where rumor spoke of lands savage with flame.<br />
He steeled himself, remembering the red<br />
Shawl with golden threads, in case of dire need—<br />
Though not yet invoked, it stayed in his stead.<br />
The valley of trials still held more deed:<br />
He entered a plain where two armies massed,<br />
Banners of hatred driving each stampede.

Caught in between, he saw spears in contrast,<br />
A battle impending for reasons lost—<br />
Old grudges, new slights, the die had been cast.<br />
Villagers fled before blades were crossed;<br />
He ran to a hillock to better view,<br />
Heart pounding for blood about to be tossed.<br />
Though no warrior, he felt he must do<br />
What he could to prevent the coming fray.<br />
But what can one man against war pursue?<br />
As horns blared and both sides formed array,<br />
He recalled the shawl of sky with gold sown—<br />
The mystic gift for despair’s darkest day.

Surely, preventing slaughter’s thunder blown<br />
Was cause to invoke whatever power<br />
Lay in that garment by sages known.<br />
He took cover behind an ancient tower,<br />
Now ruined, on that hill between the hosts,<br />
And drew from pack the blue shawl at hour.<br />
Unfurling its length, embroidered with ghosts<br />
Of symbols arcane, stars and swirling flames,<br />
He wrapped it ‘round his shoulders as war looms close.<br />
He stepped forth, heart praying in all great names<br />
Of the Beloved across creeds and lands:<br />
“One Light, guide me that hate I may tame.”

Down the slope he walked with upraised hands,<br />
Between armies now charging with fell cry.<br />
A surreal sight—one man against war’s bands.<br />
At first none noticed in battle-high,<br />
Then those at front saw a figure calm,<br />
Thinking perhaps some sorcerer is nigh.<br />
Arrows loosed from nervous archers’ palm,<br />
But strangely, each shaft curved away mid-flight,<br />
As if a wind of heaven made them qualm.<br />
The blue shawl glimmered now with inner light,<br />
Each golden thread like filaments aflame,<br />
Forming a halo brightening the dire sight.

Both sides faltered as wonder overcame—<br />
Superstitious dread that gods took part.<br />
The pilgrim seized the moment in proclaim:<br />
“Brothers! Sisters! Why tear each other apart?<br />
What demon or false fear drives you to kill?<br />
What sacred life can your sword re-start?”<br />
His voice rang out, preternaturally shrill,<br />
Amplified by shawl’s enchantment bestowed,<br />
Echoing off hills, the battle to still.<br />
The combatants, bewildered, dropped their goad<br />
A few paces from clash, unsure, amazed<br />
At this lone figure in shimmering load.

He continued, the magic in him blazed:<br />
“Look across: the face of your enemy—<br />
Is it not human, with eyes fear-razed,<br />
With heart that can bleed and dreams that can be?<br />
What do you defend that by slaughter gained<br />
Will not curse your children’s legacy?<br />
Every life lost, each soul by hate stained,<br />
Summons a grief that never will depart—<br />
Even victors are by their violence pained.<br />
Lay down your arms, listen with your heart:<br />
The Beloved has made all in one mold,<br />
What you do to another, to yourself you impart.”

Some dropped their weapons, others yet hold,<br />
But none advanced as conscience took root.<br />
Then murmurs as leaders sought to be bold—<br />
Two generals rode out to quell dispute.<br />
They met in center near where pilgrim stood,<br />
Scowling at each other in pride’s salute.<br />
“Who are you,” one barked, “who in our war intrude?<br />
Sorcerer, saint or demon? By what right<br />
Do you meddle in our people’s feud?”<br />
The other growled, “We fight for sacred right,<br />
Our cause is just, this fool best stand aside,<br />
Or by our blades he too shall feel their bite.”

The pilgrim drew a breath, pushed fear aside,<br />
His shawl’s glow flickered as doubt grazed his mind—<br />
But he anchored in faith to stem that tide.<br />
“In truth, I am no more than human kind,<br />
A wanderer who has seen much sorrow.<br />
No sorcerer nor warrior you’ll find.<br />
My right to speak is love, to peace avow.<br />
If your cause be just, test it against this:<br />
Can it stand in dialogue come tomorrow?<br />
If enmity is ancient, dare to dis<br />
Instead of bodies, break hatred itself,<br />
Find common stories where now trust amiss.”

The soldiers looked restless, the generals elf-<br />
Like cunning tried to exploit the crowd’s lull.<br />
One sneered: “His tongue bewitches like a sylph.<br />
Hear me, warriors! This trick is a gull<br />
To rob you of glory and righteous pride.”<br />
Some men roared approval, senses dull.<br />
The pilgrim realized words might slide<br />
Off hardened hearts drunk on promised reward.<br />
Perhaps demonstration must coincide.<br />
He remembered lessons in city stored—<br />
How they lit one flame from many a creed,<br />
And from emptiness synergy is poured.

He asked each general, “Do you believe in God or creed?<br />
One said ‘I honor Mars, god of war’s strife.’<br />
The other: ‘I worship justice indeed.’<br />
Then to both, pilgrim bowed, no hint of knife:<br />
“Then swear by whatever it is you adore<br />
That your cause is worth this spilling of life.<br />
If truly divine sanction you implore,<br />
Then let that divine give judgement here.<br />
Consent to a truce for just one day more.<br />
Meet in parley, let each your case make clear,<br />
Without arrow or sword, let the truth guide.<br />
If war still you choose, then resume your drear.

But if this be folly, your wrath misguided,<br />
Better to sheath swords than mothers to weep.”<br />
The armies muttered, divided, undecided.<br />
At last one captain, from silence deep,<br />
Spurred horse forward, middle-aged, battle-scarred.<br />
“I’ve seen enough blood and shallow heap.<br />
My brother died under our own bombard<br />
Last year, all for a scrap of barren land.<br />
This stranger speaks sense, though hope comes hard.<br />
I’ll stand down arms and open a hand,<br />
To talk before more sons and daughters fall.<br />
I urge all leaders to meet his demand.”

A cheer from some throats at this rebel call,<br />
And grumbles from others still bent on fight,<br />
But the momentum shifted towards sprawl.<br />
The generals, pressured by men’s weary sight,<br />
Conceded to talk under flag of truce.<br />
Day’s battle was stayed, at least for the night.<br />
The pilgrim sighed in relief at the sluice<br />
Of adrenaline drained, body now weak.<br />
The magic shawl dimmed as urgency loosed.<br />
Soldiers set camp near, exchanging wary speak,<br />
But no blood spilled and fires were shared.<br />
Small signs of trust began there to leak.

One soldier brought pilgrim water and bread,<br />
Curious what manner of man he was.<br />
Another asked for a blessing on dead.<br />
They treated him oddly with fond applause<br />
Mixed with respectful distance, as if saint.<br />
He disavowed special favor because<br />
He said the Light is in all without taint;<br />
What he did, any soul can if they choose<br />
Courage and love to let hatred grow faint.<br />
That night around campfires, news diffuse<br />
That a wanderer halted war by magic,<br />
Stories grew tall as fear did excuse.

But the pilgrim slept apart, feeling tragic<br />
That war was not over, only delayed.<br />
He prayed unity might quell conflict’s logic,<br />
But knew the morning’s parley was unmade.<br />
Before dawn, he quietly left that site,<br />
Hoping seeds of peace among them were laid.<br />
His path now led up a mountain great height—<br />
For beyond these armies a range he saw,<br />
Likely the route to his next fate aright.<br />
Though bone-tired, he climbed as new day daw,<br />
Turning back once to witness rising sun<br />
Shine on mingled tents where foes withdraw.

It gave him hope that hatred might be undone<br />
When courage and wisdom in one are spun.<br />
Thus closed the trials of the valley dun,<br />
The pilgrim battered but soul victories won,<br />
Heading now upwards to meet tomorrow’s sun.
