On a quiet dawn where valley meets wood,<br />
A traveler appeared where none had been,<br />
Along a path near where the hermit stood<br />
In earlier places, when journey did begin.<br />
This traveler walked calmly, a subtle grace,<br />
Wearing a plain brown shawl, mien genuine.<br />
Yet something luminous shone in his face,<br />
Though humble his pace, as of one who knows<br />
And needs not rush, for all is in its place.<br />
Villagers from the trial vale arose<br />
To see who approached—some recognized him,<br />
The kind stranger who eased their cruel woes.

Whisper ran through them: “It’s him, though eyes dim<br />
We thought he perished on dangerous road.”<br />
They gathered, timid joy at every limb.<br />
He greeted each by name though time had flowed,<br />
Remembering their faces, wounds and fears—<br />
They marveled: “How long our paths since you showed?”<br />
For to them, many months had passed in tears,<br />
But to him, the journey was beyond time,<br />
His transformation unbound by our years.<br />
He stayed with them again, and without chime<br />
Healed the sick with just research and a prayer,<br />
The famine ended as crops longed for life.

Rain came gentle that spring, the fields grew fair;<br />
Those bandits he’d met returned, now changed men,<br />
Bringing supplies and building homes with care.<br />
The armies from war’s brink—news traveled then—<br />
Had signed a truce, leaders influenced<br />
By a wanderer’s courage, remembering when.<br />
Our traveler worked humbly, not pretenced<br />
As holy above, but each task he met—<br />
From mending shoes to teaching with patience.<br />
Wherever he went, hearts and minds were set<br />
Alight by his presence quiet and pure,<br />
He listened deeply to all in regret.

When asked what faith he followed, to assure<br />
If he was prophet, or wizard, or saint,<br />
He answered simply, “One beyond borders.<br />
All faiths hold truth, all hearts in love acquaint.<br />
I come not to build another temple,<br />
But to help each soul from darkness to faint.”<br />
He spoke with authority yet gentle,<br />
Quoting scriptures of many a tradition<br />
With ease, making each as his own supple.<br />
Transformation came not by derision<br />
But by parables that made people think,<br />
Challenging dogmas with loving vision.

In one town’s square, near a fountain’s brink,<br />
He engaged a scholar and a priest both,<br />
On nature of truth from which all men drink.<br />
The priest insisted salvation was growth<br />
Only through his creed’s exclusive portal.<br />
The scholar argued reason was the troth—<br />
If not empirical, truth’s chaff mortal.<br />
They turned to the pilgrim for his reply.<br />
He answered with a story, not chortle:<br />
“Once a traveler, parched under midday sky,<br />
Found a well guarded by a stern old man.<br />
‘Only my bucket draws water,’ his cry.

Another wanderer came with his plan,<br />
Saying, ‘No rope but mine can reach the deep.’<br />
They argued which way was the proper span<br />
While the traveler still thirsted in keep.<br />
Tiring, he simply clambered down the wall,<br />
And drank from the source where waters do leap.<br />
Truth’s well is abundant, and gives to all;<br />
Buckets and ropes are our creeds and logic—<br />
Useful, but secondary in the call.<br />
Better we climb down to taste—to frolic<br />
In direct experience of the source,<br />
Than die of thirst amid debates prologic.”

Some folks gasped; the priest saw threat of course<br />
To authority; the scholar, offense<br />
At a truth beyond mind’s experimental force.<br />
But most laughed gently, and saw the sense:<br />
That love and direct seeking of the Real<br />
Trump quarrels over method and defense.<br />
The priest, to save face, said with wounded zeal,<br />
“So you claim all religions equal stand?”<br />
The traveler replied, “No, not ideal,<br />
For each is unique as each culture’s hand.<br />
But all hands can lift the soul to what’s right,<br />
If the heart within beats love as command.”

Rumors of a wise teacher shone bright<br />
Traveled afar to ears of city grand,<br />
And from Beloved City came invite.<br />
The pilgrim returned there at the demand<br />
Of old friends—the children now grown in grace,<br />
The elders who recalled him leaving land.<br />
He found the city flourishing, a place<br />
That had sent emissaries to all sides,<br />
Spreading seeds of knowledge, healing and place.<br />
They welcomed him with festival besides,<br />
Honoring not him (for he would not allow),<br />
But the truth he carried under disguise.

In the Temple, he spoke from oculus glow,<br />
Not from scripture scroll or doctrine’s decree,<br />
But sharing fruits of the journey’s long flow.<br />
His words juxtaposed Eastern and Western tree<br />
Into one garland of wisdom and love,<br />
Stressing virtues of integrity.<br />
He taught that the search itself is enough,<br />
That questions when honest can lead to light,<br />
That doubt can refine instead of rebuff.<br />
He urged compassion, that we must unite<br />
In care for each other, all life as kin,<br />
For the divine spark in all is alight.

There were controversial points within:<br />
He cautioned priests not to hoard divine grace,<br />
Challenged kings that the poor’s plight is their sin.<br />
Subtle his language, never naming face,<br />
But those with ears to hear knew the aim—<br />
Corrupt in power grew red in disgrace.<br />
Yet because he spoke not in anger flame<br />
But in sorrow that such potential bleeds,<br />
His censure cut deep yet left little blame.<br />
People left inspired to better deeds,<br />
Not just in awe of the mystic speaker,<br />
But resolved to plant love’s humble seeds.

After the talk, as twilight crowned with stars,<br />
He wandered to garden ‘neath olive tree<br />
Where he first had vision that healed his scars.<br />
A familiar presence then he could see—<br />
The Sufi woman, or was it the guide,<br />
Or the sage? It was all, smiling calmly.<br />
He bowed low, they embraced with no more pride<br />
Between them, for they were truly one Soul<br />
Now meeting itself on life’s other side.<br />
No words were needed; in silence whole<br />
They communicated gratitude and joy.<br />
At length the figure spoke, “The final goal

Is realized, yet work remains, dear boy.”<br />
The traveler laughed, for he was quite old<br />
In years by now, though spirit in buoy.<br />
“Yes, always the work. But with heart of gold<br />
It becomes dance and song, not mere duty.<br />
I will carry on until body cold<br />
Returns to dust. Then what comes, let it be.”<br />
The figure nodded and slowly faded,<br />
Merging into night as subtle beauty.<br />
He remained by the tree, bliss pervaded,<br />
Until a young acolyte found him there,<br />
To escort him where kindly bed was laid.

He lived among city folk for a year,<br />
Teaching by day, in meditation deep<br />
At night, bridging earth with heavenly sphere.<br />
Yet he knew soon his promise he must keep—<br />
To return to where it started, forest gloom,<br />
To the hermit or whoever gave leap.<br />
Thus one morning, quiet as dawn in bloom,<br />
He left city’s warmth for the wild again,<br />
Following memory’s signal through glume.<br />
The road seemed shorter; he knew it attain<br />
When twilight found him by the ancient wood,<br />
The same dark forest where he met the pain.

Entering beneath that canopy stood<br />
Just as in youth, but now unafraid<br />
Of shadows whispering fear as they would.<br />
He followed instinct, a spirit conveyed,<br />
To find that clearing where stone doorway gaped;<br />
He half-wondered if hermit still replayed.<br />
To his surprise, by moon’s light that draped,<br />
He found the very rock by cedar lined,<br />
But broken in two as if earth quaked.<br />
The portal was gone, just ruin assigned.<br />
He stepped into the glade and found no man,<br />
No hermit waiting with wisdom opined.

Instead, at center, a small fire began—<br />
Crackling orange, newly lit, inviting.<br />
He approached slowly, and saw of a span<br />
A circle of stones set round as writing.<br />
The fire beckoned him closer to sit;<br />
As he did so, the woods came alighting.<br />
From behind trunks, figures came to flit:<br />
The hermit in red, the hooded guide dark,<br />
The white-robed sage, the Sufi exquisite,<br />
The elder, the child, and more to remark—<br />
Faces from all moments and trials severe.<br />
They ringed him silently, each a soul-mark.

He realized these were forms held most dear<br />
In journey, but more—archetypes of soul,<br />
The Beloved’s masks to draw him near.<br />
They glowed faint, each like an aureole,<br />
Neither fully flesh nor phantom mere,<br />
As if present and absent on patrol.<br />
Then from the fire a voice crystal clear:<br />
“Thou art returned, and we are well pleased.<br />
The wood of the world thou hast carved sincere,<br />
Not into idol, but icon that eased<br />
The path for others to glimpse the divine.<br />
Thus hunger transmuted to love increased.”

All in the circle nodded in design.<br />
The pilgrim spoke out, voice echoing strong,<br />
Addressing them all or That in their sign:<br />
“It was you who guided me all along—<br />
But you are me as well, I understand.<br />
The line between self and other is gone.<br />
Thus carving the god from hunger so grand<br />
Only succeeded once I shattered the ‘I’.<br />
Now whatever you ask, I’ll do at hand.”<br />
The figures smiled in unison to imply<br />
Approval. The hermit stepped forth and said,<br />
“Your tale must be told so others might try.”

The hooded guide: “In hearts must you embed<br />
The truth that seeking is a sacred art.”<br />
The sage: “That emptiness and form be wed.”<br />
The Sufi: “That love’s fire be lit in heart.”<br />
The elder: “That wisdom with compassion blend.”<br />
The child: “That innocence and wonder never depart.”<br />
All voices together: “Go forth, dear friend,<br />
In the world of men your journey goes on.<br />
This moment closes, another will impend.”<br />
He bowed to each, then by break of dawn<br />
They vanished like mist, one final farewell.<br />
He stood alone as new day was born.

He quenched the fire and left the glade as well,<br />
Heart full, steps light, though tears in eyes did swell—<br />
Not of sorrow, but joy’s overflowing bell.<br />
He realized a circle had been traveled,<br />
From hunger to wholeness, all drama befell.<br />
A final thought in his mind unraveled,<br />
As tolling bell or last line to infer,<br />
To etch what his entire journey marveled:

“I am what happens when you try to carve God from the wood of your own hunger.”
